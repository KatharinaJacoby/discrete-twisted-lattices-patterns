### Abstract

This manuscript documents a numerical observation regarding the scaling behavior of discrete photonic lattices with anti-periodic boundary conditions ("twists"). Using a discrete Coupled Mode Equation (CME) framework, we simulated two topological configurations: the **Twisted Torus** (single twist) and the **Klein Bottle** (double twist) across a range of lattice sizes.

The primary observation is a distinct **pattern of convergence**:
1.  **Finite-Size Anomaly:** At the smallest scales, the system exhibits significant deviation from the scaling trend predicted by continuous theory.
2.  **Scale-Invariant Plateau:** Beyond a critical resolution threshold, the system settles into a stable regime where the ratio of observed critical curvature to theoretical continuous curvature becomes **constant**, regardless of further increases in lattice size.
3.  **Topology-Dependent Plateaus:** The two topologies converge to **distinct, topology-specific constant values**, suggesting the correction factor is an intrinsic property of the global boundary condition rather than a numerical artifact.

This work does not claim to have discovered a universal physical constant. Instead, it highlights a reproducible **behavioral pattern** in discrete systems: a transition from under-resolved instability to scale-invariant stability. The specific magnitude of the plateau depends on simulation parameters, but the **existence of the plateau itself** appears robust.

---

### 1. Introduction: The Discrete vs. Continuous Gap

In continuous field theory, the critical curvature $$K_c$$ of a toroidal manifold scales inversely with the characteristic length $$L$$. However, when this field is instantiated on a discrete grid, the geometry of the grid (Manhattan metric) interacts with the global topology (the twist).

This study asks a simple descriptive question: **How does the critical curvature $$K_c$$ scale as we increase the number of nodes $$N$$?**

We do not assume the answer. We simply observe the data generated by the simulation scripts.

---

### 2. Methodology and Formulas

*   **Model:** Discrete Coupled Mode Equations (CME).
*   **System:** 2D Square Lattice with nearest-neighbor coupling.
*   **Topologies:**
    *   **Twisted Torus:** Anti-periodic boundary in one dimension.
    *   **Klein Bottle:** Anti-periodic boundary in both dimensions.
*   **Metric Calculation:**
    1.  **Observed Critical Curvature ($$K_c$$):** The coupling strength where the system transitions from static to oscillatory.
    2.  **Theoretical Target ($$K_{target}$$):** The prediction from continuous theory for a loop of circumference $$L$$.
    3.  **Correction Factor ($$\alpha$$):** The ratio of the observed value to the theoretical target, representing the deviation caused by the discrete metric:
        $$ \alpha = \frac{K_c}{K_{target}} $$

*   **Verification:** We tracked **Sign Flips** (topological traversals) to ensure the system was in the correct oscillatory mode.

### 4. Pattern Analysis

#### 4.1 The Coarse-Resolution Anomaly
The most striking feature of the data is the behavior at the lowest resolution.
*   In both topologies, the coarsest lattice is an **outlier**.
*   The ratio $$\alpha$$ is different from the larger lattices.
*   The **Sign Flip** count is low, suggesting the system is "under-resolved." It lacks the geometric degrees of freedom to fully express the topological twist.
*   **Interpretation:** There is a **minimum resolution threshold** required for the discrete metric to approximate the global topology. Below this, the system is in a transitional state.

#### 4.2 The Convergence Plateau
Beyond the threshold, a clear pattern emerges:
*   The ratio $$\alpha$$ becomes **constant**.
*   This constancy holds despite a massive increase in lattice size.
*   The system is **scale-invariant** in this regime. The geometric correction is not a function of size, but a function of the **topology itself**.

#### 4.3 Topology Dependence
The two topologies do not converge to the same value.
*   **Klein Bottle:** Converges to a **distinct constant plateau**.
*   **Twisted Torus:** Converges to a **different distinct constant plateau**.
*   **Observation:** The specific value of the plateau depends on the **global boundary condition** (single vs. double twist). This suggests the "correction" is a property of how the twist interacts with the grid.

---

### 5. Discussion: What Does This Pattern Mean?

We do not claim to know the physical origin of these values. The specific numerical values of the plateaus depend on the simulation parameters (coupling strength, nonlinearity, grid spacing).

However, the **pattern itself** is robust and reproducible:
1.  **Threshold Effect:** There is a minimum lattice size required for the system to "lock in" to its topological behavior.
2.  **Scale Invariance:** Once the threshold is passed, the system behaves predictably. The geometry scales linearly, mediated by a constant factor.
3.  **Topological Signature:** The constant factor acts as a "fingerprint" for the topology. The double twist leaves a different geometric imprint than the single twist.

**Open Questions:**
*   Is the coarse-resolution deviation a fundamental limit of the discrete model, or can it be resolved with different boundary conditions?
*   Does this pattern hold for other metrics (e.g., Euclidean distance on the grid)?
*   Does the pattern persist in a continuous field model, or is it an artifact of the discrete CME?

---

### 6. Conclusion

This study documents a clear behavioral pattern in discrete twisted lattices: a transition from **finite-size deviation** to **scale-invariant stability**.

The data shows that:
*   **Coarse resolutions are special:** They behave differently, failing to sustain the full topological complexity.
*   **Beyond the threshold is stable:** The system settles into a predictable scaling regime.
*   **Topology matters:** The specific scaling factor depends on the twist configuration.

This pattern suggests that discrete systems have a **minimum resolution** required to express global topology. The existence of this threshold and the subsequent scale-invariance is the primary finding. The specific values of the correction factor are secondary and likely dependent on the specific implementation details.

Further work is needed to determine if this pattern is a universal feature of discrete field theories or specific to the Coupled Mode Equation framework.
